Vegan & Halal Substitution Guide

Learn how to prepare Korean dishes while maintaining their authentic taste and nutrition for vegan, halal, and gluten-free diets. Find substitutes for jang (fermented sauces), broth, and seasonings.

Korean Cuisine for Every Diet

Korean cuisine utilizes various cooking methods like fermentation, drying, and blanching, making it highly adaptable to dietary restrictions. The key is properly substituting jang (fermented sauces) and broth.

This guide provides practical principles and substitution strategies for adapting Korean cuisine to meet Vegan and Halal dietary requirements, while also considering common food allergies. Rather than offering fixed recipes, it focuses on ingredient-level rules and substitution logic, enabling flexible adaptation across a wide range of dishes.

üìã Core Principles

  • Grains and vegetables are generally safe for Vegan and Halal diets
  • Fermented sauces and processed seasonings require the most careful verification
  • Pork and alcohol are strictly prohibited in Halal cooking
  • Certification and labeling (Halal-certified, Vegan-certified) are essential when using processed products
  • When in doubt, plant-based alternatives and simple seasoning methods offer the safest substitution path

Substituting Jang & Sauces

How to safely select fermented sauces that are essential for umami flavor.

ü•´ Doenjang & Gochujang

  • Vegan: Use traditional meju doenjang (check for no animal additives)
  • Halal: Must be Halal-certified and produced without alcohol in fermentation
  • Gluten-Free: Confirm wheat-free doenjang
  • Substitute: Chickpea miso, tamari soy sauce

üç∂ Ganjang & Seasoning Sauces

  • Vegan: Brewed soy sauce or jin-ganjang (no animal ingredients)
  • Halal: Select halal-certified soy sauce (alcohol may form during fermentation)
  • Gluten-Free: Use tamari soy sauce
  • Low-Sodium: Low-sodium soy sauce or dilute before use

ü•Ñ Seasonings & Sauces

  • Oyster sauce substitute: Shiitake mushroom sauce, soy sauce + sugar
  • Fish sauce substitute: Dashima (kelp) broth + soy sauce
  • Saeujeot (salted shrimp) substitute: Salt + dashima broth
  • Fish stock substitute: Soy sauce + dashima powder

üö´ Alcoholic Ingredients (Haram)

  • Mirin, rice wine, cooking wine: Strictly prohibited in Halal
  • Substitute with: Vinegar + sugar, or alcohol-free Halal alternatives
  • Fermented seafood products may contain alcohol‚Äîuse Halal-certified versions
  • Always check labels for hidden alcohol content

ü•¨ Kimchi & Fermented Vegetables

  • Traditional kimchi often contains salted shrimp or anchovy sauce
  • Vegan: Substitute with vegetable stock + salt or vegan soy sauce
  • Many sauces contain hidden seafood extracts‚Äîcheck labels
  • Look for "Vegan Kimchi" products at specialty stores

Substituting Broth & Stock

How to make deeply flavored broth using plant-based ingredients.

üçÑ Making Vegetable Broth

  • Dashima (kelp) + shiitake mushrooms + mu (radish) combination
  • Use onion skins + scallion roots
  • Dried shiitake soaking water (rich umami flavor)
  • Dashima powder (instant broth)

üíß Anchovy Broth Substitute

  • Dashima + shiitake + onion combination
  • Add one spoonful of nutritional yeast
  • Dried tomato powder for umami
  • Dissolve a bit of doenjang for depth

ü•ò Bone Broth Substitute

  • Strong shiitake mushroom + dashima brew
  • Blend tofu to create creamy texture
  • One drop of deulgireum (perilla oil) for nutty flavor
  • Add ground sesame for enhanced flavor

Substituting Protein & Toppings

Introducing plant-based proteins and halal options to replace meat.

üå± Vegan Protein

  • Dubu (tofu), yeondubu (soft tofu) - versatile for all dishes
  • Tempeh (fermented soybeans, good texture)
  • Lentils, chickpeas (patties, stews)
  • Mushrooms (shiitake, oyster, king oyster)
  • Konggogi (soy meat), vegan meat (meat alternatives)
  • Nuts (walnuts, pine nuts, cashews)

üïå Halal Meat Requirements

  • Pork is Haram (strictly forbidden), including pork fat and gelatin
  • Beef, chicken, lamb: Only if Halal-certified (Zabiha slaughter)
  • Animals must be slaughtered by a Muslim, facing Mecca, with complete blood drainage
  • Halal sausages and ham available at large supermarkets
  • Always check for Halal certification mark on package labels

üêü Seafood Considerations

  • Many Islamic schools permit all seafood
  • Conservative schools: Only fish with scales allowed
  • Safe choices: Mackerel, cod, pollock (scaled fish)
  • Use dried shrimp instead of dried anchovies
  • Avoid alcohol-based marinades or seasonings

Substituting Oil & Spices

Select ingredients that control aroma and flavor according to your diet.

ü´í Oil Selection

  • Chamgireum (sesame oil) substitute: Deulgireum (perilla oil), olive oil
  • Butter substitute: Vegan margarine, coconut oil
  • Animal fats: Grapeseed oil, canola oil
  • Nutty flavor: Roasted sesame + olive oil

üå∂Ô∏è Spices

  • Black pepper substitute: Pink peppercorns, sancho (Korean pepper) powder
  • Roasted sesame salt substitute: Flaxseed, hemp seeds
  • When garlic unavailable: Use ginger powder
  • When onion unavailable: Celery, shallots

üçØ Sweetness Control

  • Honey substitute: Maple syrup, agave
  • Sugar substitute: Coconut sugar, jujube syrup
  • Mulyeot (corn syrup) substitute: Rice syrup, brown rice syrup
  • Low-sugar: Stevia, erythritol

üí° Practical Tips

Always check product labels when purchasing ingredients. It's especially important to look for vegan and halal certification marks, and verify that there are no animal-derived ingredients in the ingredient list.

  • Look for products marked 'Vegan' or 'Halal' at Asian markets
  • Completely remove moisture from frozen namul (vegetables) after thawing
  • When using substitutes, add slightly more salt and seasoning to enhance flavor
  • Start with the basic recipe and gradually adjust to your taste
